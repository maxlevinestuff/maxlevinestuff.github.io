Does it have to end?

Many millions of years ago, a discovery was made. This scientific breakthrough, the Conservation of Mass theorem, concluded “matter can neither be created nor destroyed”. Many of the individuals who heard or discovered this law didn’t care and felt indifferent. (Or they just weren't nerds.) Those who didn’t care were usually the small civilizations, which thought that there was more than enough matter in the universe to go around. For the civilizations on the higher level of the Kardashev scale, however, this news was horrible. How would they support their massive population for the rest of time without an infinite amount of resources?

A Solution Forms

The fear that was flowing through the citizens of the high level civilization caused the demand for matter to skyrocket. (Very expensive rocks.) Extraterrestrial flying asteroids, dead bodies—anything that was made of matter was pumped through the society. Even the last of the great nebulas of hydrogen began to be mined out. Everything that was made of matter was considered by the public to be good, and anything that wasn’t was considered by the public to be bad. Fear of anything that was not matter was so intense that efforts to finish populating the universe’s Great Void (a one billion light-year wide space where nothing—not even stars or even small pieces of dust—exist) had halted.

Due to the high demand in matter, a new organization was founded. The “Intergalactic Cleanup Crew (ICC)” (Could have used a better name.) was created under the idea that it would find any piece of matter that wasn’t doing anything useful—and make it do something useful. There just wasn’t enough matter in the universe to allow some of it to just sit around doing absolutely nothing. They had to make use of it. Any stray, obsolete matter would be hunted down by the ICC and taken apart at the subatomic level to be sold. They would sell billions of square kilometers worth of protons, neutrons, and electrons. (Considering these things take up no space, I'm not sure what their warehouses even looked like.)

The ICC was successful, and soon reached the company’s critical mass of over one hundred billion matter-seekers spread out across the entire universe. The matter-seekers were massive star ships that patrolled all of known existence and decided whether certain pieces of matter were worth being there or not.

Judge, Jury, Executioner

The occupants of a matter-seeker consist of two judges and a deciding committee. (We all agree that this is fair, right?) One of the judges is the defense, and the other is the offense. The defense defends the piece of matter in questions’ current position and purpose, and the offense argues that the particle(s) could be much more useful doing something else. After the two judges argue for exactly one and three hundredths of an hour, the deciding committee votes on the object’s fate. They decide whether the object will be let go to do whatever it used to be doing, or if it will be broken down to the subatomic level and then compressed in the matter-seeker’s storage compartment. The committee is usually a little bit biased, however, as they receive a five percent commission of however much the subatomic particles sell for. (This is actually incredibly generous.)

Over a century ago, a matter-seeker known specifically as 1AS21 was patrolling an insignificant part of the western spiral arm of a galaxy sometimes referred to as “the Milky Way”. Little is known about why the inhabitants decided to name their galaxy “the Milky Way”, but some theories suggest that it is referring to a white substance excreted from some of the inhabitants. Whatever the reason for the name of the galaxy, 1AS21 was patrolling it. Inside the insignificant western spiral arm of the Milky Way, the 1AS21 was patrolling an even more insignificant eight-planet solar system. Three planets from this solar system’s sun were a blue-green planet harboring the life of the same species of idiots who decided to name the galaxy in which they lived “the Milky Way”. Six hundred miles from the surface of the blue green planet hovered 1AS21, making its decision on the planet’s ultimate fate. In exactly one and three hundredths of an hour, the planet would either remain untouched or would be completely dismantled into electrons, protons, and neutrons and stuffed into the matter-seeker’s storage compartments. A Billion Lives, a Drop of Fuel

“Welcome to the court of matter-seeker 1AS21,” began the offense judge. “The planet earth of the Geniva solar system in the western spiral arm of the milky way galaxy inside the intergalactic Virgo supercluster has been charged for the following crime: existing without purpose. Is their anything that the defense would like to say to disprove that earth is guilty of this crime?” The defense cleared its throat. (Yes, please aim the microphone at us, like what was done in Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy.)

“The earth is a planet with life. Allowing it to stay in existence could offer a future food source to our civilization,” said the defense.

“Are you not aware that the life forms on earth are carbon based?” asked the offense. “Carbon is not fit to be eaten by our species, which disallows the possibility of becoming food.”

Bureaucratic Difficulties

The defense rolled its eyestalk, (Actually very fashionable among alien species.) showing that it didn’t care whether the earth was destroyed or not. “The earth is at optimum distance with its moon, which means great beaches with perfect waves. Because of the moons distance from the earth and the pull on the oceans it causes, we could turn earth into a vacation surfing area,” said the defense.

“Slippery slope!” called the offense. “We are neither talking about the moon nor the oceans, we are talking about the earth—stay on topic.”

The defense was bored. This was its eleventh planet in one day, and it wanted to go home. It glanced at its watch—damn, only five minutes had passed since the start of earth’s conviction.
